Period 4

1800 - 1848
Starting with Jefferson’s election during the Revolution of 1800, the early/mid 19th century saw the effects of the idea of manifest destiny and the growth of more sectional differences in the United States partly as a result of that idea. Thomas Jefferson was a Democrat Republican who favored states’ rights, wanted to limit federal power, and supported a strict interpretation of the US Constitution. However a lot of his actions during his presidency were inconsistent with those beliefs. For example, during the Louisiana Purchase, he went against his belief in a strict interpretation of the Constitution in order to double the size of and benefit the nation. Jefferson’s presidency also eventually led the Era of Good Feelings during James Monroe’s presidency, since Jefferson’s term saw the dwindling of Federalist power and the shift to a one-party system. The Era of Good Feelings, named because there was a sense of peacefulness due to there being just one political party, started after the War of 1812 against Britain ended. The War of 1812 created a sense of nationalism within the country and eventually led to the Industrial Revolution. The Federalist party died after the War of 1812 because of the Hartford Convention. However, there were still some Federalists in the Supreme Court, as seen by the expansion of federal power resulting from Supreme Court cases like Marbury v. Madison, that established judicial review, McCulloch v. Maryland, that said states couldn’t tax the national bank, and Gibbons v. Ogden, which said that only the federal government could regulate interstate commerce. Meanwhile, big social movements like the Temperance movement, women’s rights movement, and education reform started during this time period, along with the rise of the slavery debate again. The slavery of issue was previously ignored for fear of breaking the nation’s unity, but as America continued to expand and new territories were acquired, the issue of slavery couldn’t be ignored. The divided opinion about slavery, along with increased sectional differences due to the industrialization of American society, would soon lead to the Civil War.

Turning Points
1
Marbury v. Madison
  • Causes:
    • ​John Adams tried to install new justices at the last minute of his presidency
  • Description:
    • ​Marbury was appointed to be a justice in the last days of Adams' presidency
    • Madison, Jefferson's Secretary of State, refused to send the order of the justice
    • Petition on behalf of Marbury and other justices ensued and Marbury insisted Marshall to order Madison to send the commission
    • Madison refused and the case went to the Supreme Court
  • Effects:
    • ​​Established judicial review
2
Louisiana Purchase
  • Causes:
    • ​​Napoleon needed money and decided to sell a large amount of land for money
    • Jefferson wanted to buy land 
  • Description:
    • ​James Monroe and Robert Livingston negotiated a deal to buy the territory for $18 million
    • There was a huge dispute over the Louisiana Purchase
      • Jefferson believed in a strict interpretation of the Constitution, but still bought the Louisiana Purchase
  • Effects:
    • ​Expansion of the US
    • Loose interpretation of the Constitution
3
War of 1812
  • Causes:
    • Macon's Bill #2
    • ​Britain attacked Americans ships
  • Description:
    • A war between the U.S. and Great Britain caused by American outrage over the impressment of American sailors by the British, the British seizure of American ships, and British aid to the Indians attacking the Americans on the western frontier
  • Effects:
    • ​End of the Federalist party
      • Era of Good Feelings begins
    • National anthem created
    • Andrew Jackson becomes a war hero, helping him with the election
4
Monroe Doctrine
  • Causes:
    • ​After the war, Monroe decided it was too risky to be involved in European affairs
  • Description:
    • ​​One of the first real U.S. foreign policy decisions made by the new country
    • James Monroe asserted that any attempts to colonize the Western Hemisphere would be met with American intervention
  • Effects:
    • ​Policing later in Latin America
    • Used by many other presidents for justification of the spread of U.S. influence in the western hemisphere
5
Missouri Compromise
  • Causes:
    • ​Dispute over slave states and free states
  • Description:
    • ​Missouri wanted to join as a slave state, which create an imbalance between the slave and free states
    • Maine, another state wanting to join, could join as a free state, therefore balancing it
    • States above the 36' 30 line would join as free states while states below that would join as slave states
  • Effects:
    • ​Kept the Union together for many years
    • Led to the:
      • Kansas-Nebraska Act
      • Civil War

Synthesis
Henry Clay's American System was made of SIT: the Second bank of the US, Internal improvements, and Tariffs. Alexander Hamilton's Financial Plan was made of BEFAT: Bank of the US, Excise tax on whiskey, Funding at par, Assumption of state debts, and Tariffs. Both Clay's and Hamilton's plans involve federal banks and tariffs that would strengthen the national government.

Andrew Jackson was a Democratic-Republican who was voted into office in 1828, representing the "common man" and valuing federal power over state power. Theodore Roosevelt took over the presidency after William McKinley was assassinated and created the Square Deal, allying himself with progressives who urged regulation of corporations. Neither Jackson nor Roosevelt were afraid of big businesses and corporations, fought for the "common man," and greatly expanded the power of the federal government.

The Antebellum Era was the time period before the Civil War during which there were many reforms, including the establishment of public schools, improving the treatment of the mentally ill, controlling and/or abolishing the sale of alcohol, winning equal rights for women, and abolishing slavery. The Civil Rights Movement refers to the strategies, groups, and social movements in the United States whose goals were to end racial segregation and discrimination against African Americans and to secure their rights. Both involved fighting for the rights of those, like African Americans who were being treated unjustly and were being oppressed, and both wanted reform.
