Period 7: Part 1

1890 - 1920
Prior to this time period between 1890 and 1920, rapid industrialization, urbanization led to the growth of big business and corruption within the industries and politics, so the next time period showcased progressive movements that tried to enact change to get rid of corruption and the like. There were many social reform movements during this time, started mainly due to the publishings of muckrakers like Upton Sinclair, who wrote “The Jungle” and exposed the horrors of the meat industry, and Jacob Riis, who published “How the Other Half Lives” to show the terrible living conditions within tenements. Muckrakers were journalists who exposed issues to bring about change. Thanks to the efforts of muckrakers, more people started demanding reform. Some big issues that progressives fought for included women’s suffrage, civil rights, education reform, and child labor. Then Theodore ”Teddy” Roosevelt took office. With his Square Deal that promised corporate control, conservation, and consumer protection, Teddy Roosevelt greatly expanded the power of the federal government, creating legislation that regulated and controlled the industries and breaking up trusts and monopolies. Also during this time period, the US was increasing its presence as a world power. Since the United States was now functioning fairly well on its own, they could afford to focus more on foreign affairs. Imperialism started during this time period, since the idea of manifest destiny led to the United States expanding coast to coast and they could now only expand to areas overseas. During the Age of Imperialism, we gained more territories in areas like the Philippines, Puerto Rico, American Samoa, and Guam after the Spanish-American War. Since we gained more territory, debates once again started over what the United States should do with these newly-acquired territories. As America asserted itself as a world power, other countries were also imperializing. This would later on lead to World War I.

  • Causes:

  • Description:

  • Effects:
    • ​​Stopped several conflicts over land
    • Gave the U.S. more trading influence

  • Causes:
    • ​​Sherman Antitrust Act didn't do anything
    • Corruption
    • Exploitation of workers
  • Description:
    • Extension of the trust-busting that had been done earlier by Teddy Roosevelt
    • Exempted labor unions from being labeled as trusts
    • ​Lengthened the Sherman Antitrust Act's list of prohibited practices
    • Made sure that labor unions had the right to strike and hold peaceful picketing events
  • Effects:
    • ​​Limited the power of corporations over their employees

Synthesis
The Chinese Exclusion Act outlawed Chinese immigration for 10 years and explicitly denied naturalization rights to Chinese in the U.S. The National Origins Act restricted immigration from any one nation to two percent of the number of people already in the U.S. of that national origin in 1890, which severely restricted immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, and excluded Asians entirely. Both of these acts showed nativism and restricted immigration, especially Asian immigration.

The Progressives were people during the Progressive Era who advocated for economic, social, and political reform. The Brain Trust refers to specialists in law, economics, and welfare, and many young university professors, who advised President Franklin D. Roosevelt and helped develop the policies of the New Deal. Both of these parties wanted reform and helped bring change in terms of practically every aspect of American life.
