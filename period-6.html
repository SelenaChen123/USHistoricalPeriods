Period 6

1865 - 1898
After the Civil War ended, the United States entered a time period known as the Gilded Age. Appropriately named the Gilded Age by writer Mark Twain, this time period was characterized by a seemingly golden period of rapid economic growth, industrialization, and urbanization, along with underlying corruption in both businesses in politics. The Gilded Age saw lots of new inventions, such as the Henry Ford’s assembly line and Henry Bessemer’s steel-making process, and the booming of the railroad industry contributed greatly to the urbanization and industrialization of the nation. Big businesses/corporations were created during this time period, with people like Andrew Carnegie, who monopolized the steel industry, and John D. Rockefeller, who dominated the oil industry coming to power. The wealth gap increased greatly as big corporations were created and more and more immigrants started coming to the US due to America’s rapid industrialization. Immigrants and laborers were being exploited because labor was cheap, so labor unions like the American Federation of Labor and the Knights of Labor were created. Workers started protesting, going on strikes, and demanding more government regulation of the industries to protect workers’ rights. This would later lead to the Progressive movement, as Americans started wanting more government regulation to fix economic and social issues.

Turning Points
1
Transcontinental Railroad Built
  • Causes:
    • ​The need for faster transport and a connection from across the country
  • Description:
    • ​Completed in 1869 at Promontory, Utah, it linked the eastern railroad system with California's railroad system, revolutionizing transportation in the west
    • Credit Mobilier arose as a scandal during its building
  • Effects:
    • ​Made transportation faster and easier
    • Took away land from Native Americans and caused some of their deaths
    • Led to more industrialization
2
Pullman Strike
  • Causes:
    • ​Eugene V. Debs lowered wages
  • Description:
    • ​A strike of workers for the Pullman Palace Car Company
    • President Cleveland ordered 2,000 troops to stop the strike and said he had this power because the strike interfered with mail
  • Effects:
    • ​Set a precedent for the government to interfere on the side of business and not the worker.
3
Haymarket Riot
  • Causes:
    • ​​Demand for an eight-hour workday
  • Description:
    • ​Happened in Haymarket Square of Chicago during a strike for an eight-hour workday
    • A bomb was thrown at police and killed eight people
  • Effects:
    • ​Led to increased divisions in the country
    • Some people such as labor organizers saw those involved as martyrs and others thought that the strikes were an unjustified disruption of business by foreigners leading to an increase of xenophobia
4
Dawes Severalty Act
  • Causes:
    • ​Ghost Dance Movement
    • A Century of Dishonor by Helen Hunt Jackson
  • Description:
    • ​​Meant to assimilate the Native Americans more quickly into American Society
    • It was to give each family 160 acres of land and grant them citizenship
    • It also sent many Native American children to schools
    • However most of the land was never given out and almost half of Indian land was lost to white settlers due to this act
  • Effects:
    • ​Native American tribes were broken up
    • Native Americans were recognized as individuals instead of tribes
5
Sherman Antitrust Act
  • Causes:
    • The push for regulation of monopolies
  • Description:
    • ​​​Banned monopolies
    • Cooperations got around this act by forming holding companies
    • The courts didn't enforce this
  • Effects:
    • ​Expanded with the Clayton Anti Trust Act
    • There wasn't much of an effect since it wasn't really enforced

Synthesis
Henry Ford created the assembly line and the first mass-produced car, the Model T.  William Levitt used mass production techniques to build inexpensive homes in surburban New York called Levittowns. Both of these people helped create products that were once very expensive for average Americans, rather affordable.

The transcontinental railroad was completed in 1869 in Promontory, Utah, and it linked the eastern railroad system with California's railroad system, revolutionizing transportation in the west. The National Interstate and Defense Highways Act constructed 40,000 miles of interstate highways over a 10-year period. Both of these revolutionized and increased the use of transportation.

Andrew Mellon's Plan said that high taxes forced the rich to invest in tax-exempt securities rather than factories that provided prosperous payrolls. Reagonomics refers to the federal economic polices of the Reagan administration that was based around supply-side economics, or the theory that allowing companies the opportunity to make profits and encouraging investment will stimulate the economy and lead to higher standards of living for everyone.  Both of these gave the rich tax cuts and thought that money would flow from the rich to the poor in doing so.
